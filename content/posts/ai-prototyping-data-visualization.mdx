### Let's start with a simple data visualization using **Claude** chatbot.

I have a CSV containing around 50,000 cross-border transactions with columns such as when was the transaction initiated by the customer, when did the beneficiary receive the funds, the destination & source currency, amount, and fees. There is a derived field, settlement time, based on the timestamp columns in the raw data. It essentially signifies how much time it took for a transaction to settle and the value could be instant, <1 hour, <3 hours, <6 hours, <12 hours, 24-72 hours. 

The full CSV can be accessed via this link: [cross\_border\_transactions\_20250128\_213446](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1xLsdrnNyRzy7wmT3BvtaGUTs25K6QoUg7wtrKY-3OuA/edit?usp=sharing)

It’s a pretty sophisticated visualization with just 1 line of prompt honestly! It shows the % distribution of the volume of a particular currency based on its settlement time. For example - Major currencies (USD, EUR, GBP) have:

-   Higher percentages of instant settlements (25-30%)
-   Faster overall settlement times
-   Lower percentages of 24-72 hour settlements

Whereas, currencies like INR show:

-   Lower instant settlement percentages (10%)
-   Higher percentages of longer settlement times
-   More even distribution across the middle categories

Pretty neat!

Claude presented several insights to my prompt.

1.  Most Expensive Currency Pairs:
    -   USD-INR has the highest average fees ($45.2)
    -   GBP-INR and EUR-IDR follow closely
    -   Generally, corridors involving emerging market currencies have higher fees
2.  Least Expensive Currency Pairs:
    -   EUR-GBP has the lowest average fees ($20.5)
    -   USD-GBP and USD-EUR also have relatively low fees
    -   Major currency pairs consistently show lower fees
3.  Fee Consistency:
    -   Fees are NOT constant for any currency pair
    -   There's significant variation between minimum and maximum fees
    -   Factors affecting fees include:
        -   Transaction size (larger transactions tend to have higher absolute fees)
        -   Market conditions
        -   Time of day/market liquidity
        -   Payment method
        -   Correspondent bank relationships
4.  Fee Structure Insights:
    -   Base fees exist for all transactions
    -   Fees scale with transaction size but not linearly
    -   Major currency pairs have smaller fee ranges
    -   Emerging market corridors show wider fee ranges

You can also try to modify the fee visualization a bit by asking Claude to

“**_Add additional metrics like fee as a percentage of transaction value_**” OR

“**_Break down the fees into different components (correspondent fees vs. other fees)_**”.

So hopefully you get the gist. A small side note that the transaction data is also generated from Claude. I gave it a crisp prompt and it delivered the CSV exactly how I wanted. The _**prompt**_ was:

**_Generate a csv containing transactions._**

Claud generated the code which I then ran in [JupyterLab](https://jupyter.org/try-jupyter/lab/). My python notebook threw several errors related to package installation errors or other random environment errors. Claude tried its best to fix the errors but what amazed me was its ability to adjust and pivot with every error. Super impressed!
